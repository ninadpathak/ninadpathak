## The programmer who became a writer

I started my career writing code. I learned to read documentation, curse at it when it was wrong, and appreciate it when someone actually explained the hard parts.

Then I started writing, bringing everything I learned from the engineering side with me.

That transition is the whole point. Most technical writers approach a codebase the way a tourist approaches a city, everything looks right on the surface, but locals can always tell. Engineers at every company I've worked with have spotted the same thing. When someone who actually understands the technology writes about it, the output is different. Developers feel it.

That's my edge. I don't just explain. I verify. I run the code. I check the API. I notice when the example in the docs matches the current version.

## What I work on

I write for **DevTools and B2B SaaS companies** whose audience is developers, engineers, or technical decision-makers. The work usually falls into one of three categories:

- **Technical tutorials and implementation guides**, step-by-step walkthroughs where the code actually runs and the concepts are explained at the right level of depth
- **Developer-facing blog content**, long-form articles on AI agents, testing infrastructure, localization workflows, code review tools, and similar topics that live at the intersection of engineering and product
- **Content strategy and SEO**, building topical authority for technical products through systematic, intent-driven content programs

## What the results look like

**Kiwi Sizing** went from 7,000 to 450,000 monthly visits, a 64x increase, through programmatic SEO and topical content.

**DelightChat** grew from zero to 50,000 monthly organic visits through a ground-up content strategy.

These metrics reflect treating content as a product, with the same rigor you'd apply to an engineering project.

## How I work

I keep my client list small deliberately. When I take on a project, I'm actually thinking about it, reading the docs, testing the product, understanding the audience. That's not possible at scale.

I'm responsive, I meet deadlines, and I'll tell you when something is wrong with the brief rather than writing around it. Editors describe my work as requiring minimal revision. Engineers describe it as accurate.

That combination is harder to find than it should be.
