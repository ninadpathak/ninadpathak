Engineers write documentation. They write blog posts. They write release notes. This works until it does not.

Know the signs that you need dedicated technical writing help.

## Documentation Always Ships Late

If docs are consistently the bottleneck before releases, you have a capacity problem. Engineers view documentation as blocking work. Technical writers view it as primary work.

Dedicated writers align documentation with release schedules naturally.

## Your Content Sounds the Same

Every post reads like an engineer wrote it for other engineers on their team. Jargon goes unexplained. Context is assumed. Tone varies wildly.

Technical writers write for audiences. They adjust for beginners versus experts. They maintain consistent voice.

## Code Examples Have Bugs

Readers report that your tutorials do not work. Dependencies changed. APIs evolved. No one updated the posts.

Technical writers test code meticulously. They maintain working repositories. They verify every release.

## You Are Not Publishing Consistently

Your blog has three posts from six months ago, then silence. Your documentation has gaps where features launched without explanation.

Technical writers operate on editorial calendars. They deliver predictable output. They fill gaps systematically.

## Your Content Does Not Convert

Traffic exists but trials do not increase. Readers bounce after reading. No one clicks your calls to action.

Technical writers understand conversion. They write product led content. They create natural bridges from education to evaluation.

## Engineers Hate Writing

They complain about documentation tasks. They rush through them. The quality shows their reluctance.

Forcing unwilling engineers to write produces poor results. Let engineers engineer. Let writers write.

## You Need Multiple Content Types

You need documentation, blog posts, whitepapers, video scripts, and email newsletters. Each requires different skills.

Technical writers often have range across formats. They adapt content for different channels efficiently.

## What to Look For

Hire for:
* Writing samples in your domain
* Experience with your tech stack
* Portfolio showing measurable results
* Ability to interview engineers effectively
* Understanding of SEO basics

Do not hire based solely on technical credentials. Writing skill matters more than engineering experience. Domain knowledge can be learned. Writing craft takes years to develop.

## Start With a Contract

Test the relationship before committing to full time. Hire a freelance technical writer for three months. Evaluate output quality. Evaluate process fit. Evaluate impact on metrics.

Successful contracts convert to permanent roles. Unsuccessful contracts end cleanly.
