Engineers know things worth sharing. They built the system. They solved the problems. They have war stories.

Your job is extracting that knowledge. Turning it into content. Making it accessible.

Here is how to interview engineers effectively.

## Do Your Homework First

Read their code. Read their documentation. Read their previous talks or posts.

Know their work before you ask about it. This respects their time. It enables deeper questions.

## Record Everything

Do not rely on notes. Do not try to capture quotes live. Record the conversation. Transcribe it later.

Tools like Otter.ai or Zoom transcription work well. You want exact wording. Engineers use precise language.

## Ask For Stories

Bad question: "How does the caching layer work?"
Good question: "Tell me about a time the cache failed in production. What happened? How did you fix it?"

Stories contain drama. Drama creates engagement. Technical details emerge naturally from narrative.

## Dig Into Decisions

Every system represents dozens of decisions. Ask about alternatives considered. Ask about tradeoffs evaluated.

Example: "Why Redis over Memcached? What would have made you choose differently?"

These answers reveal engineering thinking. They teach readers how to evaluate their own choices.

## Get Specific Numbers

General statements are weak. Specific data is strong.

Push for numbers:
* "How much did latency improve?"
* "What was the error rate before the fix?"
* "How long did the migration take?"

Numbers make claims credible. Numbers help readers benchmark their own systems.

## Clarify Jargon Immediately

Engineers speak in acronyms. They mention internal project names. Stop them. Ask for explanations.

If you do not understand it, your readers will not either. Clarify for yourself. Then clarify in the final content.

## Ask About Mistakes

Everyone loves a good failure story. Ask what went wrong. Ask what they would do differently.

Vulnerability builds trust. Lessons from failure stick better than success stories.

## Validate Quotes Before Publishing

Send relevant sections to the engineer before publishing. Confirm technical accuracy. Confirm you captured their intent.

This prevents misquotation. It builds trust for future interviews.

---

Good interviews extract gold. They surface insights the engineer forgot were valuable. They create content no competitor can replicate because no one else has access to your source.
